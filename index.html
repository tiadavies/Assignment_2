Greener Cities, Healthier Lives

Exploring the global connections between urban green space, health, and development from 2000–2010.

Understanding the Key Variables

The indicators below provide essential context for interpreting the visualisations that follow. They connect environmental quality, health outcomes, and economic development—revealing how urban design and social investment shape human wellbeing across the globe.

  • Green Area per Capita: Portions of urban land covered by long-term vegetation such as trees, shrubs, forests, or perennial grasses that remain green for most of the year. Waterbodies are not included.
  • Life Expectancy: The average number of years a person is expected to live in years.
  • Alcohol Consumption: Average number of litres of pure alcohol consumed per person.
  • Percentage Expenditure on Health: Total expenditure on health as a percentage of GDP per capita.
  • Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria Immunisation: Immunisation coverage among 1 year olds.
  • Body Mass Index (BMI): The average BMI across the population.
  • Gross Domestic Product (GDP): Expressed in US dollars
  • Schooling: The average number of years of formal education completed per person.

Global Overview

How are green spaces and life expectancy distributed across the world? These maps provide an overview of global disparities and their evolution in 2000 and 2010.

Environmental and Health Patterns

Between 2000 and 2010, global green area per capita declined across nearly all regions. Urban expansion and land-use change reduced the availability of vegetated land in many countries, especially in developed urban centres. The United States saw one of the most pronounced declines, with average green area dropping from around 580 m² to roughly 420 m² per person, reflecting the growing pressure of urbanisation on natural and recreational spaces.

In contrast, life expectancy generally rose during the same period, highlighting continued improvements in global health and living standards. Advances in healthcare systems, vaccination programs, and education likely contributed, even as environmental pressures intensified. Many nations in Europe, Asia, and Latin America experienced steady gains in longevity.

However, this pattern was not universal. Russia and Libya both recorded a decline in life expectancy in 2010 compared to 2000, pointing to regional instability, health crises, or socioeconomic disruption during that decade. These exceptions remind us that health outcomes are influenced by factors beyond environmental or wealth indicators alone.

Green Space and Longevity Insights

Across both years, countries with both high and low levels of green area show similar life expectancies. Regions such as Northern America and Europe and Australia and New Zealand consistently maintain higher life expectancies regardless of green area, suggesting other factors like healthcare, income, and education play stronger roles.

In contrast, Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Central and Southern Asia display lower life expectancies even when green space is relatively available, indicating that green area alone does not directly translate to better health outcomes. Between 2000 and 2010, life expectancy increases modestly across most regions, pointing to global improvements in living conditions and healthcare rather than changes in environmental space per person.

Body Mass Index Distributions

Variations in health outcomes are shaped not only by environment but also by lifestyle. Here we explore BMI distributions around the world.

Australia and New Zealand and Northern America and Europe display the highest median BMI values, suggesting higher rates of overweight and obesity, which may increase risks for chronic diseases such as diabetes and heart disease.

In contrast, Central and Southern Asia, Eastern and South-eastern Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa show lower BMI levels, potentially reflecting undernutrition or limited access to high-calorie foods. Latin America and the Caribbean and Western Asia and Northern Africa fall in the mid to upper range, indicating emerging health challenges linked to dietary transitions.

Education, GDP, and Development

Economic and educational development underpin improvements in both health and sustainability. The following charts compare schooling years and GDP across developing and developed nations in 2000 and 2010.

Education and GDP Growth

In 2000, the relationship between percentage expenditure on education and average years of schooling shows a clear divide between developed and developing countries. Developed nations generally had higher average schooling, with Australia leading at 20.4 years. Developing countries varied more, with Niger at the bottom, averaging only 2.8 years, despite some nations spending significant GDP shares on education.

By 2010, the trend persisted: developed countries maintained high schooling levels, with New Zealand at the top at 20.4 years. Most developing nations made moderate gains, reflecting gradual improvements in education systems. However, disparities remain, highlighting persistent global inequality in educational attainment.

Niger still had the lowest average schooling in 2010, at 4.5 years, showing that increased global investment does not always translate into higher outcomes. These charts underscore how both expenditure and governance factors influence the effectiveness of educational investment.
